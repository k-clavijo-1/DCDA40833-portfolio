AI Tool Evaluation

Introduction

In this lab, I evaluated two AI tools—ChatGPT and Leonardo AI—using a structured framework focused on capabilities, appropriate use, ethics, and transparency. The purpose of this analysis was to critically assess how AI tools can support academic and professional work while still requiring human judgment, verification, and responsible disclosure.

Tool 1: ChatGPT

Capabilities

ChatGPT was found to be highly effective in text generation, summarization, and structural support across a variety of tasks. When asked to explain complex concepts related to digital ethics and data analysis, the tool produced well-structured responses that were clear and easy to understand. It was also useful in generating the initial HTML skeleton for a portfolio page, which saved time during early development. However, ChatGPT showed weaknesses when asked for specific sources or current factual information. Some responses sounded confident but required external verification, highlighting the tool’s tendency to favor fluency over accuracy. Output quality varied significantly depending on prompt clarity.

Appropriate Use

ChatGPT is most appropriate for early-stage ideation, outline creation, summarizing readings, and assisting with code structure. It functions best as a support tool that improves efficiency rather than replacing original thinking. Poor use cases include relying on the tool for final analytical conclusions, factual authority, or unattributed academic work.

Ethical Considerations

ChatGPT is trained on a mixture of licensed data, data created by human trainers, and publicly available information. This raises concerns related to bias, authorship, and originality. While safeguards exist to reduce harmful content, accuracy is not guaranteed. Ethical use requires transparency, verification, and disclosure when AI assistance is involved.

Tool 2: Leonardo AI

Capabilities

Leonardo AI proved effective in generating visually compelling images for creative and marketing-related purposes. When prompted to produce Gen Z– focused advertising visuals and conceptual illustrations, the tool generated polished images suitable for mood boards and early creative brainstorming. However, it struggled with fine details such as readable text and realistic human features. Repeated prompts sometimes produced visually similar results, suggesting limits in originality without extensive refinement.

Appropriate Use

Leonardo AI is most effective for visual ideation, concept development, and creative inspiration. It is best used during the brainstorming phase of a project rather than for final branded assets or client-ready designs. Human designers and strategic judgment are still required to refine and contextualize AI-generated visuals.

Ethical Considerations

Ethical concerns include intellectual property, originality, and representation. Because Leonardo AI is trained on large image datasets, there is a risk of style imitation and unclear ownership of outputs. Biases in representation may also appear. Responsibility ultimately lies with the user to ensure ethical, transparent, and culturally sensitive use.

Broader Reflections

I view AI tools as supportive collaborators rather than replacements for human work. I would use ChatGPT for brainstorming, outlining, and efficiency, and Leonardo AI for visual inspiration during early creative exploration. Human judgment, revision, and disclosure remain essential to maintain integrity and originality.

In the DCDA context, AI tools can increase efficiency and experimentation, but overreliance may weaken critical thinking and analytical depth. Skills that remain uniquely human include ethical judgment, cultural interpretation, strategic decision-making, and contextualizing data and visuals within real- world settings.

As AI tools evolve, important evaluation questions include how the tool was trained, what biases or limitations exist, whether outputs can be verified, and what ethical responsibilities users hold.
